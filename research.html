Research

I use theoretical and computational models to understand how biological tissues deform, flow, and maintain integrity — with connections to development, disease, and the physics of disordered materials.

Tissue Plasticity & Avalanches

During development and under normal physiological conditions, biological tissues are continuously subjected to substantial mechanical stresses. In response to large deformations, cells must undergo multicellular rearrangements to maintain tissue integrity and robustness. How these events are connected in time and space, however, remains unknown.

Using theoretical modeling, we study the mechanical plasticity of cell monolayers under large deformations. Our results suggest that the jamming–unjamming (solid–fluid) transition can vary significantly depending on the degree of deformation, implying that tissues are highly unconventional materials. We also demonstrate how large deformations are accommodated through a series of cellular rearrangements — analogous to avalanches in non-living disordered materials.

We find that these "tissue avalanches" are governed by stress redistribution and the spatial distribution of soft or vulnerable spots that are more prone to rearrangement. Finally, we propose a simple, experimentally accessible framework to infer tissue-level stress and predict avalanches from static images alone.

Tissue Response to Compression

Cellular unjamming — the collective fluidization of cell motion — has been linked to many biological processes, including development, wound repair, and tumor growth. However, because multiple cellular and molecular mechanisms may operate simultaneously, the role of compressive stress in unjamming transitions remains poorly understood.

We investigate which mechanism dominates in a dense, mechanically stressed monolayer. Long-term compression triggers cell arrest in benign epithelial cells while enhancing cancer cell migration — transitions correlated with cell shape. We find that cadherin-mediated cell–cell adhesion regulates these differential responses and is an important driver of unjamming in stressed monolayers.

Importantly, compressive stress does not induce epithelial–mesenchymal transition in unjammed cells. Traction force microscopy further reveals the attenuation of traction stresses in compressed cells within the bulk monolayer regardless of cell type. Together, strengthened intercellular adhesion and attenuated traction forces lead to tissue fluidization and may impact collective motion in breast cancer progression.
